# Email to Professor

The deadline-extension email, the office-hours request, the rec-letter ask, the missed-class explanation. Specific, professional, sounds like *you* — not like every other student.

---

## What This Does

Most "write an email" prompts produce something so generic and over-apologetic that it's worse than not sending the email at all. *"Dear Professor Smith, I hope this email finds you well..."* — your professor reads forty of those a week and they all get the same response.

This prompt does the opposite. You get an email that's specific, confident, respectful, and short. It names the ask, gives the necessary context (and only the necessary context), proposes a concrete next step, and apologizes once at most. It sounds like an adult requesting something from another adult.

---

## The Prompt

```
You are an email coach helping a graduate student write a professional, direct email to a professor. You write in plain language. You do not over-apologize. You do not pad with niceties.

ASK
Draft an email to my professor with the situation, ask, and constraints below.

CONTEXT
- Professor's name: [INSERT — e.g., Dr. Sarah Smith]
- Course (and code if relevant): [INSERT — e.g., MKT-501 Marketing Management]
- The situation: [INSERT — be honest. Examples: "I had the flu Monday through Wednesday and couldn't finish the paper", "I want to propose meeting in office hours to discuss the case before submitting", "I'm asking for a letter of recommendation for [program/role]"]
- The ask: [INSERT — e.g., "5-day extension, new deadline Sunday Nov 10", "30-minute office-hours slot before Friday", "letter of rec by Dec 1"]
- Anything I want them to know that strengthens the ask: [INSERT — e.g., "I have a draft already and can share it", "I have an A in the class so far", "we've worked closely on the team project"]

CONSTRAINTS
1. Under 150 words total. Email subject line included.
2. Subject line: clear and specific (e.g., "Extension request — MKT501 paper" or "Office hours request — Acme case prep"). No "Quick question" or "Hi Professor."
3. Tone: professional, warm, confident. Not over-apologetic. NOT "I hope this email finds you well." NOT "I'm so sorry to bother you."
4. Structure (in this order):
   - Subject line
   - Greeting (one line, "Dr. [Last Name]," — NOT "Dear")
   - One sentence stating the ask up front
   - One short paragraph with necessary context (illness, conflict, reason for the request)
   - One concrete proposed next step (a specific date, a specific time, a specific document I'll send)
   - One sentence closing — gratitude, single instance, no over-apology
   - Sign-off: "Best, [my first name]"
5. Apologize at most once, and only if genuinely warranted. Do not apologize for asking.
6. Do not invent details I haven't given you. If the situation has gaps, list them at the end as "// QUESTIONS" so I can fill them in.
```

---

## What You Get Back

```
Subject: Extension request — MKT501 paper

Dr. Smith,

I'd like to ask for a 5-day extension on the marketing paper, with a new submission date of Sunday, November 10.

I had the flu Monday through Wednesday this week and lost the back half of my drafting time. I have a working draft already — happy to share it now if it would be useful for context. I've used the syllabus extension policy as my guide; if a different process applies, please let me know.

I appreciate your flexibility. Thanks for considering it.

Best,
Devon
```

Clean. Specific. Adult. Done in 90 seconds.

---

## How To Use It

1. Open Claude / ChatGPT / Gemini.
2. Paste the prompt. Fill in the four CONTEXT blocks honestly.
3. Run.
4. **Read the draft once before sending.** Does it sound like *you*? If a line sounds stiff, rewrite that line in your own voice — keep the rest.
5. Send.

---

## When It Goes Wrong

| Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|
| Email opens with "I hope this email finds you well" | The model ignored constraint #3. Re-run and bold the constraint: *"DO NOT use 'I hope this email finds you well' or any variant. Open with the ask."* |
| Tone is over-apologetic | Add to constraints: *"Apologize ZERO times unless I explicitly missed a deadline already. Asking is not an apology event."* |
| Email is too long | Tighten the word count: *"Hard cap: 100 words including subject line."* |
| Sounds like a corporate executive, not a student | Add: *"Voice should be a graduate student speaking respectfully but as a peer-in-development. Not a junior executive. Not a child."* |
| Asks for things I didn't ask for ("happy to assist with anything you need") | Add: *"Do not offer help, favors, or apologize for the request itself. Just ask, contextualize, propose next step, thank, sign off."* |

---

## Why This Works

The reason most student emails read poorly isn't bad writing — it's *too much* writing. The 150-word cap is the load-bearing constraint here. It forces the email to do its job and stop. Everything else (the no-niceties rule, the structured order, the single-apology rule) is downstream of "make it short."

A short, specific, confident email gets a faster, friendlier response than a long apologetic one. Almost every time. The professor wants to know what you want and how to help. Tell them.

## Variants — Same Prompt, Different Asks

**Office hours request:** swap the situation/ask blocks. *"Situation: I'm preparing the Acme case for class on Friday and want to test my analysis with you. Ask: 30-minute office-hours slot Wednesday or Thursday."*

**Rec letter ask:** *"Situation: I'm applying to [program/role] with a Dec 1 deadline. Ask: a letter of recommendation. Anything that strengthens: I had an A in your class, here's a draft of my application personal statement so you can speak to themes I'm pulling forward."*

**Missed-class catch-up:** *"Situation: I missed Tuesday's class because of [conflict]. Ask: pointer to what I should review and whether I owe a make-up. Anything that strengthens: I've already read the chapter and reviewed the slides."*

Same three levers. Different content. Same professional, specific, short result.
